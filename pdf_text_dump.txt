1. Title
Working Title: Evaluating the Impact of Workplace Wellness Programmes on Government HR Efficiency and
Performance: A Study of Madhya Pradesh Civil Services.
2. Background
The performance of government institutions is intrinsically linked to the health and vitality of its human re‐
sources. Public sector employees, particularly those in Human Resource (HR) departments, operate within
a high‐pressure environment characterized by rigid bureaucratic structures, heavy workloads, and a high
volume of public interface. Despite the critical nature of their roles, there remains a persistent gap in the
implementation of holistic wellness frameworks within the Indian civil services. Traditional approaches have
largely focused on curative healthcare—addressing illness after it occurs—rather than proactive, preventa‐
tive wellness that focuses on the overall quality of life (WHO, 1948).
In the Indian context, the Central Government Health Scheme (CGHS) has been the primary vehicle
for employee medical assistance since 1954, providing comprehensive coverage for more than 4 million
beneficiaries ( CGHS Portal ). However, these schemes are often medical‐centric, focusing on reimbursement
and hospitalization rather than continuous wellness monitoring and stress mitigation. The World Health
Organization (WHO) defines a Healthy Workplace as one where workers and managers collaborate in a
continual improvement process to protect and promote the health, safety, and wellbeing of all workers
(WHO Healthy Workplace Framework, 2010 ). This framework identifies four ” Avenues of Influence”: the
physical work environment, the psychosocial work environment, personal health resources, and enterprise
community involvement.
Currently, government HR officials in Madhya Pradesh—ranging from Class I to Class IV—face diverse
stressors. While higher‐level officials deal with policy‐level complexities and long‐duration meetings, lower‐
tier officials handle administrative bottlenecks and clerical fatigue. This study seeks to evaluate how these
varied wellness challenges impact departmental efficiency. By integrating global standards like the WHO
Healthy Workplace Model with national efforts such as the Ayushman Bharat Digital Mission (ABDM) and
theNational Mental Health Policy , this research explores the feasibility of transitioning from a ’sick‐care’
model to a ’well‐care’ model within the government hierarchy.
3. Rationale and Need for Study
While the Government of India has robust statutory requirements under the Occupational Safety, Health
and Working Conditions Code (2020) , these are primarily compliance‐driven and focused on hazardous
industries. There is a significant lack of data regarding the ”psychosocial wellness” of administrative HR
staff. The need for this study arises from:
•High Absenteeism and Presenteeism: Measuring how hidden health issues lead to decreased produc‐
tivity even when staff are ”at their desks.”
•Administrative Burnout: Investigating the psychological toll of rigid deadlines and bureaucratic pressure
on HR officials.
•Inadequacy of Current Schemes: Moving beyond the CGHS curative model to identify if preventative

4. Research Objectives
1.Evaluate the baseline wellness levels of Class I, II, III, and IV government HR officials using WHO
parameters to substantiate the need for structured interventions.
2.Analyze the impact of workplace stressors on operational efficiency, providing a reasoning for the
implementation of departmental wellness protocols.
3.Substantiate how age and gender‐based wellness differences correlate with performance, allow‐
ing for the usage of targeted health initiatives.
4.Develop an evidence‐based framework that demonstrates the correlation between holistic well‐
ness investments and increased public service productivity.
5. Conceptual Framework
This research builds on a few key concepts and theories:
Wellness and Health
According to the World Health Organization (WHO), health is not just about not being sick. It is a state
of complete physical, mental, and social well‐being.
Dimensions of Wellness
We will also use Dr. Bill Hettler’s idea that wellness has multiple parts, like our physical health, our
emotions, and how we feel about our jobs. If one part is suffering, it affects everything else.
Efficiency and Performance
We will look at how taking care of employees helps them deal with the heavy demands of their jobs,
so they can perform better and get more done (efficiency).

This study adopts a Mixed‐Methods Research Design , combining quantitative rigor with qualitative depth
to capture a holistic view of employee wellness.
6.1 Data Collection Sources
•Primary Research: Direct engagement with 200 HR officials across Class I‐IV in Bhopal and surrounding

6.2 Quantitative Research (Structured Questionnaire)
A structured questionnaire will be administered to all participants, using Likert‐scale questions and multiple‐
choice options to ensure statistical validity.
•Closed‐Ended Questions: Focused on WHO parameters: Physical health (sleep, diet), Mental health (fre‐
quency of stress), and Environmental health (ergonomics).
•Demographic Variance: Capturing age and gender to correlate wellness outcomes with departmental
roles.
6.3 Qualitative Research (In‐Depth Interviews)
To understand the ”why” behind the data, semi‐structured interviews will be conducted with select officials.
•Open‐Ended Questions: ”Describe the most stressful part of your workday,” or ”How does your current
state of health impact your decision‐making speed?”
•Factors Included: Influence of hierarchy on mental stress, peer‐support systems, and perceived organi‐
zational culture.
6.4 WHO vs National Parameters
The study will prioritize WHO Healthy Workplace Framework parameters. While National (GoI) parameters
focus on safety and compliance (Occupational Safety Code, 2020), WHO parameters offer a more holistic
view including:
•Psychosocial Environment: Organizational culture, management styles, and work‐life balance.
•Personal Health Resources: Access to fitness, nutrition, and mental health support.
Note: Where WHO data is unavailable for specific local contexts, Indian National Health Mission (NHM)
baseline indicators for civil servants will be utilized.
7. Government Initiatives in Health & Wellness

•AYUSH in Wellness: Promotion of Yoga and Ayurveda in government offices ( Ministry of AYUSH ).
•ABHA (Ayushman Bharat Health Account): Digital health records for seamless care ( ABDM Portal ).
•National Mental Health Programme (NMHP): Focused on stress management and de‐stigmatization.
•Mission Karmayogi: National program for civil service capacity building including health as a pillar of
performance.

8. Significance / Expected Contribution
This research will highlight the real, everyday struggles of government HR officials. It will show the true
value of having wellness programmes in government offices. By understanding what these employees
need, we can help the government create a healthier workplace, which will lead to a faster and better‐
performing public service.
9. Scope & Limitation
Scope: This study focuses closely on Class I, II, III, and IV government HR officials in Madhya Pradesh and
how their personal wellness ties back to their efficiency at work.
Limitation: Because this is early exploratory research using a small sample size from specific classes, our
findings will give us good ideas and a starting point, but they may not apply to all government departments
or other states.
10. Prior Studies & Literature Review
A review of landmark studies demonstrates the varying impacts of workplace wellness programmes across
both corporate and public sectors:
•Illinois Workplace Wellness Study (RCT, 2019): A large‐scale randomized controlled trial by Jones, Moli‐
tor, and Reif evaluated nearly 5,000 university employees. Although health screening rates increased,
the study found no significant causal effects on health behaviors, medical expenditures, or absenteeism
after two years, highlighting strong selection bias (healthier employees were more likely to participate).
•RAND Corporation Study (2013): Mandated by the Affordable Care Act, Mattke et al. found that while
participants showed statistically significant improvements in exercise, smoking cessation, and weight
control, there were no substantial reductions in overall medical costs or cholesterol.
•Baicker, Cutler & Song Meta‐Analysis (2010): A Harvard meta‐analysis of over 30 studies suggested that
for every dollar spent on wellness programmes, medical costs and absenteeism costs fell by $3.27 and
$2.73 respectively. Most analysed programmes focused on multiple risk factors like smoking, obesity,
and stress in large firms.
While these studies highlight mixed results—varying strictly by methodology and intervention type—
they also underscore the importance of robust measurement indices and the need for rigorous study de‐
signs.
11. Wellness Domain Parameters
This study adopts the WHO Healthy Workplace Model integrated with the widely recognised Eight Dimen‐
sions of Wellness framework (SAMHSA, 2016). While national indicators focus on statutory safety, the WHO
parameters allow for a more holistic evaluation of the psychosocial environment. For each dimension, we
identify key indicators that will guide our primary data collection.

11.1 Physical Wellness
Physical – Body Health & Activity
•Blood pressure, cholesterol, BMI (biometric screening results)
•Frequency of physical activity per week
•Quality and duration of sleep
•Nutritional habits and dietary patterns
•Sick leave and absenteeism rates
•Prevalence of chronic conditions (diabetes, hypertension)
11.2 Emotional Wellness
Emotional – Stress & Resilience
•Self‐reported stress levels (1–10 scale)
•Burnout risk assessment scores
•Ability to cope with daily work pressures
•Utilisation of Employee Assistance Programme (EAP) or counselling
•Emotional self‐awareness and self‐regulation
11.3 Intellectual Wellness
Intellectual – Learning & Growth
•Participation in professional development and training programmes
•Engagement in creative problem‐solving at work
•Satisfaction with opportunities for skill development
•Pursuit of new knowledge or certifications
11.4 Occupational Wellness
Occupational – Job Satisfaction & Balance
•Employee engagement and job satisfaction survey scores
•Work‐life balance self‐assessment
•Perceived alignment between personal values and job role
•Manager support and recognition feedback
•Retention and voluntary turnover rates

11.5 Spiritual Wellness
Spiritual – Purpose & Meaning
•Sense of purpose and meaning in daily work
•Alignment with organisational mission and values
•Engagement in mindfulness, meditation, or yoga practices
•Participation in community service or volunteer activities
11.6 Social Wellness
Social – Relationships & Community
•Quality of interpersonal relationships with colleagues
•Perceived sense of belonging within the workplace
•Frequency of peer‐to‐peer recognition and support
•Participation in team‐building and social events
•Availability of a personal support network
11.7 Financial Wellness
Financial – Security & Planning
•Satisfaction with current salary and benefits
•Usage of financial planning tools and retirement schemes (NPS, GPF)
•Financial stress and its impact on work focus
•Awareness of employee welfare entitlements
11.8 Environmental Wellness
Environmental – Workspace & Safety
•Workplace safety and ergonomics assessments
•Satisfaction with office infrastructure (lighting, ventilation, cleanliness)
•Access to clean drinking water and sanitation facilities
•Availability of a dedicated break or wellness room
•Reported workplace safety incidents
